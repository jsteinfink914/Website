Onside Kick vs. 4th and 15: An optionality Proposal for the NFL

 

Introduction

            With the game on the line and down by one possession after scoring a touchdown in the NFL, a team has minimal opportunity to get the ball back without much time going off the clock. In fact, there is only one option open to a team to regain possession immediately: the onside kick. When a team does this, the kicker attempts to kick the ball more than 10 yards (to avoid a penalty) but close enough where their own team can recover the ball before the opposing team does. When expecting it, the opposing team will go into an onside kick formation and stack their players in the likely recovery zone. One can imagine that the probability of executing this desperation play is low, and this is true as it converts on average 13.3% of the time since 2012, according to my own analysis. However, this is no longer the case since the rules changed in the 2018 NFL season. In an effort to reduce concussions and make kickoffs safer in general, the NFL decided to no longer allow the kicking team to get a running start through the 35 yard line, and instead they must remain stationary until the ball is kicked.[1] To be sure, this policy has been effective as it has led to a 38% reduction in concussions suffered on kickoffs.[2] However, it also neutralized the effectiveness of the onside kick. More numbers will be discussed later, but the conversion rate has dropped significantly since the change, from 15.1% to 10.5%.

            Knowing of this effect, the Philadelphia Eagles proposed a possible rule change in 2019 and again in 2020. They wanted to allow teams to run a 4th and 15 play from their own 25 yard line in lieu of an onside kick.[3] Importantly, the play would be an untimed down and if a penalty hurting the offense were to occur (imagine a5 yard false start penalty) the team would still have to carry out the attempt from their new down and distance (in this case, a 4th and 20 from their own 20 yard line). The proposal has been tabled both times, but there has been discussion on it, and some think it will provide an injection of excitement to late-game situations. This document aims to add further credence to the proposal.

Methodology

            To perform the analysis on the onside kick, 4th down option policy, the open-source programming software R was used. Data was pulled from the nflreadr library which is a package meant for easy access to the nflverse project, a set of packages meant to provide data for the NFL.[4] Using this package, play-by-play data could be obtained. For the project, data from 2012-2021 NFL seasons was collected (2012 was when the kickoff yardline was pushed forward from the 30 to the 35 and touchbacks were moved from the 20 to the 25 yard line).

To isolate onside kick data, every play (row entry) has a column called “play type.” Taking only plays with “kickoff” as the play type and containing the word “Onside”  in the description column allowed for the isolation of the 609 onside kick attempts since 2012. For determining whether or not the onside kick was recovered, the description column, thankfully, contained the word “RECOVERED” in all caps if the kick was indeed recovered. This signal

as used to create a binary variable where 1 equals a recovered onside kick and 0, a non-recovered onside kick.

To isolate 4th down data, a column called “down” was used which stored the current down. Taking only the rows where down equals 4 yields 41,192 4th down attempts since 2012. From there, 5 yard intervals were taken and used for the analysis (4th and 1-5, 6-10,11-15,16-20,20-25). Importantly, many 4th down plays are punts, so it was critical to filter out plays that were not “pass” or “run” plays as listed in the “play type” column. Lastly, a column called “first down” represents a binary variable where 1 indicates a successful first down, and 0 a turnover on downs.

Results

            The first step in performing the analysis is to look at onside kicks, both the number of attempts and conversion rates over time.

 






In the first chart we see that onside kick attempts have remained relatively consistent, around 60 each year. The rule changes in 2018 did not seem to have much of an effect on attempts, which makes sense; a desperate team is still going to attempt an onside kick no matter the odds because it is their only option. However, in the bottom chart one can see that after the rule change, the onside kick conversion rate seems to drop. To see if this is significant, a hypothesis testing procedure must be performed where the null hypothesis is that the onside kick conversion rates before and after the rule change are equivalent.



A z-score of 1.96 represents a 5% probability the two are equivalent, while a z-score of 1.645 represents a 10% probability. The z-score in this case is 1.7166, which is significant at the 10% level and with more samples could be significant at the 5% level. The difference in mean conversion rate pre 2018 and post 2018 is 4.67%, as it dropped from 15.1% to 10.5%.

Switching gears to 4th down data, groups were made in 5 yard intervals from 4th and 1-5 to 4th and 20-25. Conversion rates and attempts are plotted below.


Unsurprisingly, as the yardage increases, attempts and conversion percentage drop precipitously. For the purposes of this analysis, it is clear that only 4th and 15-20 and 20-25 are worth considering by virtue of the conversion percentages in comparison to onside kicks, 16.4% and 10.5% respectively. 

            Now a comparison of the 4th down situations of interest and onside kick rates must be performed. The same procedure that was used to compare pre and post 2018 onside kick rates will be used here. A t-test is a simple tool, but very powerful for showing whether or not two distributions share a statistical mean.





Both 4th and 15-20 and 20-25 both cannot be ruled out as having equal means as that of the new onside kick conversion rate. For context, since 2018 there have been 239 onside kicks whereas there have been 140 4th and 15-20 and 57 4th and 20-25 attempts since 2012. Both 4th down scenarios are not statistically significantly different at either the 95% or 90% level, although 4th and 15-20 is quite close (1.61 vs. 1.645).


           



Moreover, the same test provides similar results when comparing against pre-2018 onside kick rates. Given that the pre-2018 rate is higher than post-2018 the rightward shift within the t-score distribution is unsurprising, as the Onside kick conversion rate was greater.

In this comparison, we see that both 4th down scenarios are not statistically significantly different from pre-2018 onside kick rates, and this can be said with more confidence for 4th and 15-20 attempts now then with post-2018 onside kick rates.

            According to Bizzozero, surprise and suspense are statistically significant predictors of audience size. Additionally, he found that “both suspense and surprise generate even more entertainment value during a match’s later moments, i.e., when the stakes are higher.”[5] Buraimo et. al, supports these findings and determines that there is an economically significant effect that comes from comparing an “exciting” game to a traditional one in terms of audience size.[6]

Interpretation

            These results indicate the usefulness of a potential 4th and 20 option in the NFL. Since rule changes in 2018 prevented kicking teams from getting a running start, the onside kick conversion rate has dropped significantly (from 15% to 10%). After investigating 4th down scenarios, specifically from 15-20 yards and 20-25 yards, it appears that both of these options are statistically similar in terms of conversion rate when compared to the new rate of onside kick success. In fact, it appears that these options resemble more closely the success rate of onside kicks before the rule change. Given that the rule change was meant to limit concussions and not to limit late game theatrics, lowering the probability of the “desperation chance” was not the intended effect.

            Given that NFL teams make the majority of their revenue from ticket sales,[7] a key driver of revenue growth is increased TV ratings and viewership. More viewers means more advertising dollars, and both surprise and suspense have been shown to be correlated with increased TV ratings. As a result, improving the likelihood that surprising and suspenseful situations will take place can lead to significant revenue inflows. Additionally, it has been shown that commercials will be more effective following surprising and suspenseful moments, and as a result these slots can be sold for more money.[8] Thus boosting late game surprise and suspense is one of the most economically efficient ways to boost TV revenue as the marginal effects at the end of games loom the largest. By allowing for a 4th and 15 attempt, surprise and suspense will increase as late game comebacks will have a higher probability ascribed to them. Beyond simply improving revenue, the fan experience may benefit from the prospect of more intense competition.

Conclusion

            Money matters in the NFL and TV dollars are the main source of revenue. Improving TV ratings presents a clear pathway towards improving revenues and increasing surprise and suspense in games is a sound strategy to utilize in pursuit of this goal. Introducing a 4th and 15 option in lieu of an onside kick will return to the losing team an opportunity to make a late game comeback with similar likelihood as the pre-2018 onside kick while potentially boosting TV revenues. This proposal has the potential to benefit owners, players, and fans. As a result, a one year trial period in which teams can take the 4th and 15 option should be considered.
